**The Diocese of Kansas City–Saint Joseph is a mid-size Catholic diocese managing $144.5 million in combined assets across 80 parishes, 31+ schools, and 27 administrative offices spanning 27 counties in northwestern Missouri.** This dossier assembles the organizational, financial, operational, and industry data needed to build a McKinsey-caliber Strategic AI ROI Report. ## 1. Organization overview

### What the diocese is

The Diocese of Kansas City–Saint Joseph is a Latin Church diocese of the Roman Catholic Church in northwestern Missouri, a suffragan diocese within the ecclesiastical province of the Archdiocese of Saint Louis. It was **formally established on August 29, 1956** by combining two predecessor dioceses—the Diocese of St. Joseph (founded 1868) and the Diocese of Kansas City (founded 1880)—giving the combined entity roughly **70 years of operation** and a Catholic heritage in the region dating to 1723.

The diocese serves approximately **151,000 Catholics** within a total regional population of ~1.55 million people across **27 counties** covering **15,429 square miles** of western and northwestern Missouri. The geographic footprint stretches from the Kansas City metropolitan area north to the Iowa border, encompassing both densely urban parishes and remote rural missions. Counties served: Andrew, Atchison, Bates, Buchanan, Caldwell, Carroll, Cass, Clay, Clinton, Daviess, DeKalb, Gentry, Grundy, Harrison, Henry, Holt, Jackson, Johnson, Lafayette, Livingston, Mercer, Nodaway, Platte, Ray, St. Clair, Vernon, and Worth.

## 5. Industry context: Catholic diocese / religious nonprofit

### The clergy crisis defines the operating environment

The U.S. Catholic Church faces a structural workforce crisis that is the single most important contextual factor for AI ROI analysis. **Active priests fell from 58,000 in 1970 to under 35,000 by 2025**, while the Catholic population grew from 50 million to 73.5 million. The average age of U.S. priests is now **63 years old**. Ordinations have dropped from 805 annually (1970) to roughly **451**. Priestless parishes climbed from 549 in 1965 to over **3,496** by 2012, and the trend has only accelerated. Over **60% of priests under 45** report burnout. For the Diocese of KC–SJ specifically, 97 diocesan priests serve 88 parish/mission locations—a ratio that leaves little margin.

### Attendance and engagement are declining sharply

**Only 17–24% of U.S. Catholics attend Mass weekly** (CARA 2025 data), down from majority attendance a generation ago. Approximately **300,000–350,000 Catholics leave the Church annually**. The Diocese of Buffalo reports 60% of parishioners are over age 60 and 49% of parishes are declining in registered households. Parish closures and mergers are accelerating nationally: Buffalo is merging ~34% of its parishes, Pittsburgh permanently closed 7 churches in March 2026, and Columbus, Baltimore, Erie, Peoria, and Joliet have all announced restructuring.

### Financial pressures are mounting

Catholic dioceses face a convergence of financial pressures. Fundraising nationally shows stress: only **19% of first-time donors** returned in 2024, and affluent household giving participation fell from 91% (2015) to **81% (2024)**. Deferred maintenance on aging parish and school buildings is a pervasive challenge. Underfunded priest pension and post-retirement health obligations are common. Abuse settlement costs have exceeded **$3 billion** nationally. Top-performing nonprofits allocate 15–25% to administration; Catholic dioceses typically target similar ratios but face upward pressure from shrinking parish assessment bases.

### Peer dioceses are beginning to engage AI

Several Catholic institutions have started formal AI engagement:

- **USCCB Fall 2025 Plenary**: U.S. bishops received a formal AI briefing from a Notre Dame theology professor covering benefits and risks for diocesan operations, schools, and healthcare
- **Maryland Catholic Bishops**: Issued a formal pastoral letter on AI, pledging ongoing formation, support for Catholic technologists, and pro-common-good AI legislation
- **Diocese of Toledo**: Staff actively exploring AI with a published Catholic perspective
- **Vatican**: Enacted AI governance law (January 1, 2025), issued "Antiqua et Nova" doctrinal note on AI, and leads the "Rome Call for AI Ethics" (signed by Microsoft, IBM, Cisco, Salesforce, and 21 world religions)
- **Pope Leo XIV** has made AI governance an early priority of his papacy, drawing on Leo XIII's advocacy for workers during the Industrial Revolution

Catholic-specific AI tools now commercially available include **Magisterium AI** (28,000+ Church documents, used by clergy and theologians globally), **Pushpay/ParishStaq AI** (giving data analysis, AI-powered people search, ML fraud detection), and **Truthly** (Catholic faith Q&A). Pushpay's platform includes patent-pending AI features to convert one-time givers to recurring donors.

## 6. AI opportunity framing for the diocese

### Where AI creates the most value for a diocese like KC–SJ

Nonprofit AI adoption has reached **82–89%** in some capacity (though deep integration remains uncommon), with **77% reporting noticeable improvements**. Organizations using AI for fundraising report **20–30% increases in donations**. AI automation saves an average of **15–20 hours weekly** on administrative tasks. Positive ROI typically emerges within **6–12 months**. Here are the highest-value application areas ranked by potential impact for this diocese:

**Donor and development operations (highest ROI potential)**: Predictive analytics for donor identification and major gift prospecting could transform the Annual Catholic Appeal and Bright Futures Fund. AI-powered donor segmentation, churn prediction, and personalized outreach at scale could drive material revenue increases. One unnamed diocese used AI data analysis to turn a projected **$250K campaign goal into $750K–$1M**. AI-enhanced average donations run **$161 vs. $115 industry average** for one-time gifts.

**Communications and content creation (fastest time-to-value)**: Parish bulletin drafting, Catholic Key content, social media management, stewardship curriculum development, and multilingual translation (critical for Hispanic Ministry) represent quick wins. A Catholic diocese generated a complete K-8 stewardship curriculum using AI, saving **10–20 hours of research per project**. With 80 parishes, Catholic Charities, and multiple offices all producing communications, content generation AI could have outsized impact.

**Finance, accounting, and compliance**: Automated financial reporting across 80+ separately incorporated parish entities, budget forecasting, giving pattern analysis, cross-entity consolidation, and audit preparation. The finance office currently has a small team managing complex multi-entity finances—automation could meaningfully expand capacity.

**Parish administration**: AI-powered people search across sacramental records, natural language data queries ("show me all families who haven't attended in 6 months"), volunteer scheduling, event management, and faith formation registration. With 88 parish/mission locations operating semi-independently, standardized AI tools could reduce duplicated administrative effort.

**HR and volunteer management**: Staff scheduling optimization across multi-parish clergy assignments, onboarding workflow automation, ministry team coordination, and benefits administration. The diocese's 5-person HR team manages benefits for a workforce potentially numbering in the thousands.

**School operations**: Enrollment management, personalized parent communications, tuition payment tracking, MOScholars program administration, and faith formation milestone tracking across 31+ schools.

### Strategic positioning

The Diocese of KC–SJ is well-positioned for AI adoption for five reasons. First, the diocese has operated a **balanced budget for 33+ consecutive years**, meaning AI investment can be framed as capacity-building rather than crisis response. Second, the **Vatican and USCCB have explicitly endorsed** ethical AI use, removing theological risk as a barrier. Third, the **clergy shortage** makes administrative efficiency existential—every hour saved in admin is an hour available for ministry. Fourth, the diocese's **complex multi-entity structure** (80 parishes, 31+ schools, Catholic Charities, cemeteries, and 27 chancery offices all operating semi-independently) creates significant coordination overhead where AI-driven standardization could generate outsized returns. Fifth, **Catholic-specific AI tools now exist** (Magisterium AI, Pushpay ParishStaq, Truthly), reducing the implementation risk and theological concerns that might accompany secular AI tools.

## Conclusion

This dossier reveals a diocese with **solid financial foundations** ($144.5M in combined assets, 33 years of balanced budgets), **significant operational complexity** (88 parish/mission locations, 31+ schools, Catholic Charities, 27 chancery offices), and **no formal AI strategy**—against a backdrop where 82–89% of comparable nonprofits have begun AI adoption. The structural pressures of priest shortage, declining attendance, and fundraising headwinds are not unique to KC–SJ but create urgency for efficiency gains. The highest-ROI AI opportunities cluster around **donor/development operations** (where 20–30% fundraising increases are documented) and **administrative automation** (15–20 hours/week saved), with Catholic-specific tools now commercially ready. The diocese's recently strengthened financial oversight (post-embezzlement) and ongoing deanery restructuring create a natural inflection point for technology modernization. The Vatican's explicit endorsement of ethical AI use removes what might otherwise be the most significant adoption barrier for a Catholic institution.
