# Midwest Trust: CAIO Strategic Growth Research Brief

**Midwest Trust Company is a 32-year-old, privately held trust and wealth management firm headquartered in Overland Park, Kansas, managing roughly $8 billion in direct assets as part of a broader $500 billion platform — positioning it squarely in the mid-market sweet spot where AI adoption is lowest and transformation upside is greatest.** The company sits within MTC Holding Corporation, a family of eight affiliated entities spanning personal trust, institutional custody, retirement plans, and self-directed IRAs. The wealth management industry faces a structural inflection: a projected shortage of 100,000+ advisors by 2034, fee compression squeezing margins, and client expectations shifting decisively toward digital-first experiences. Trust companies like Midwest Trust lag RIA firms in AI adoption by roughly 4:1 — creating both urgent vulnerability and significant first-mover advantage.

## Industry pressures demanding an AI response

The wealth management and trust industry faces four converging pressures that make the status quo unsustainable for mid-market firms.

**The advisor talent crisis is existential.** McKinsey projects a shortage of **90,000–110,000 financial advisors by 2034**, representing roughly a third of current headcount. Cerulli Associates reports **109,093 advisors** plan to retire in the next decade — 37.5% of headcount controlling 41.5% of industry assets. The average advisor is 51 years old. The rookie failure rate sits at **72% within five years**. Only 6% of retiring advisors have a fully documented succession plan. For a trust company with 25-year-average tenure staff, this is not a theoretical risk — it is an operational countdown. AI is the only realistic path to maintain service capacity without proportional headcount growth.

**Fee and margin compression is structural.** Mutual fund expense ratios have dropped **61% since 1996** (from 1.04% to 0.40%). Active management fees are projected to reach 0.44% by 2025. PwC projects that profit as a share of AUM has already fallen 19% since 2018 and will decline another 9% by 2030. The median RIA operating margin is **27.8%**, but smaller firms (under $1B AUM) see margins as low as 18%. Operational costs per $1B of AUM are projected to reach **$2.81M by 2030**. Trust companies cannot out-fee this trend — they must out-efficiency it.

**Regulatory and compliance costs keep rising.** Compliance costs have increased **60%+ relative to pre-financial crisis levels**. Trust companies face a uniquely layered regulatory environment: state trust regulations, SEC oversight (if RIA-registered), BSA/AML requirements, FATCA/CRS, fiduciary duty standards, and now emerging AI governance rules. EY reports **86% of wealth/asset management firms** cite regulatory complexity as the greatest hurdle to AI adoption — paradoxically, the very complexity driving costs is also the barrier to the technology that could reduce them.

**The Great Wealth Transfer will punish analog firms.** An estimated **$84–105 trillion** is shifting to younger generations, and **81% of heirs plan to fire their parents' advisor** after receiving an inheritance. Among millennials, 73% communicate with advisors primarily through digital channels. Only 18% of full-service firms' apps deliver a proactive, interactive experience. Trust companies with paper-heavy, relationship-only models risk losing the next generation entirely.

## How competitors are already deploying AI

The gap between AI leaders and laggards in wealth management is widening rapidly. **68% of RIA firms** now report using AI in some capacity, but trust companies adopt AI at roughly **one-quarter the rate** of independent RIAs — making this the industry's most underpenetrated segment.

**Morgan Stanley** is the clear industry benchmark. Its GPT-4-powered AI Assistant, drawing on 350,000+ proprietary documents, has achieved **98% adoption** among financial advisor teams. The firm's AI Debrief tool auto-summarizes client meetings and drafts follow-ups, saving advisors up to **15 hours per week**. Morgan Stanley attributed AI-augmented workflows to generating **$64 billion in net new assets** in Q3 2024 alone. **Goldman Sachs** has deployed a firm-wide multi-model AI assistant across all employees, routing tasks to the most effective LLM by function. **JPMorgan** has given 60% of employees GenAI access. **HSBC's** "Amy" processed over 3 million investment research reports in Q1 2024 alone.

At the mid-market level, **95% of wealth and asset management firms** have now scaled GenAI to multiple use cases (EY 2025). The most common deployments are meeting summarization and note-taking (highest adoption, freeing 8–10 hours/week per advisor), research synthesis and knowledge retrieval, client communication drafting, compliance monitoring, and tax-loss harvesting optimization. Regional firms like **Savant Wealth Management** ($30B AUM, Illinois) are early movers, with leadership predicting complete industry transformation within 3–5 years.

## Where AI fits Midwest Trust's operations

A mid-size trust company like Midwest Trust runs six core operational domains — each with significant manual burden and clear AI intervention points.

**Client onboarding** involves KYC/AML screening, trust document review, beneficiary setup, and account opening across multiple custodian platforms. Trust-specific KYC is uniquely intensive, requiring identification of settlors, trustees, beneficiaries, and protectors. Firms typically use 200+ step checklists, and onboarding can take weeks. **AI opportunity:** Document extraction (OCR + NLP) can parse trust instruments to automatically identify key provisions, beneficiaries, and distribution standards — reducing weeks of manual legal review to minutes. Automated KYC/AML screening with real-time risk scoring can compress the compliance bottleneck.

**Trust administration** — the firm's core differentiator — remains heavily manual. Distribution processing currently takes approximately **15 hours per distribution** due to data gathering across siloed systems, trust document interpretation, committee approvals, and audit documentation. Annual trust reviews require narrative summaries across investment performance, distribution activity, and compliance status. **AI opportunity:** AI agents can automate trust document parsing, distribution calculations, and compliance checking — reducing prep time by up to 80%. Auto-generated annual review summaries can free trust officers to focus on fiduciary judgment rather than data assembly.

**Compliance and regulatory reporting** spans state trust regulations, SEC requirements, BSA/AML, and fiduciary standards — with data scattered across CRM, trust accounting, document management, and email. Audit preparation is particularly time-consuming when records live in multiple systems. **AI opportunity:** Continuous regulatory monitoring, auto-classification and archiving of client communications, real-time transaction surveillance, and on-demand audit-ready report generation.

**Portfolio management and reporting** uses platforms like Orion, Black Diamond, or Tamarac for rebalancing, performance measurement, and client statements. Quarterly commentary and client-specific presentation materials are typically drafted manually. **AI opportunity:** Auto-generated personalized portfolio commentary, real-time drift detection, proactive tax-loss harvesting alerts, and predictive rebalancing.

**Client communications** include quarterly reports, annual reviews, ad-hoc beneficiary requests, and coordination with external attorneys and CPAs. Much of this remains manual — including firms that still print and mail quarterly statements. **AI opportunity:** AI-drafted personalized quarterly narratives, automated meeting prep materials, and intelligent chatbot/concierge features for 24/7 portal responsiveness.

**Document management** is a universal pain point. Trust documents, estate filings, tax records, and correspondence are typically scattered across network drives, email, trust accounting systems, and physical files. **AI opportunity:** AI-powered classification, extraction, and indexing can transform unstructured document repositories into searchable, structured knowledge bases — making audit preparation a minutes-long query rather than a days-long dig.

## Conclusion: the strategic case for AI at Midwest Trust

Midwest Trust has the profile of an ideal AI transformation candidate: a **knowledge-intensive, people-dependent business** with highly credentialed but aging talent, manual-heavy trust administration workflows, a fragmented technology environment, and active growth through acquisitions that compound integration complexity. The company's standalone revenue ($15M–$22M) and headcount (100–140) place it in the mid-market tier where **AI adoption is lowest but productivity leverage is highest**.

Three framing themes for the strategic growth report stand out. First, **AI as a capacity multiplier** — with the advisor talent crisis making it impossible to scale through hiring alone, AI is the only viable path to increasing households-per-advisor without degrading service quality. Second, **AI as a margin defense** — fee compression and rising compliance costs will erode profitability unless operational efficiency improves dramatically; the target should be moving from an estimated 18–25% operating margin toward the 30%+ tier through automation of the manual trust administration burden. Third, **AI as a generational bridge** — the $84 trillion wealth transfer will flow to digitally native heirs who expect real-time portals, personalized reporting, and instant responsiveness; firms without AI-powered client experiences will lose the next generation of trust relationships.

The competitive window is narrowing. Morgan Stanley's tools are already generating measurable asset growth. Trust companies lag RIAs 4:1 in AI adoption. For Midwest Trust, the question is not whether to adopt AI but how fast it can move from laggard to leader in a segment where early movers will capture disproportionate advantage.
