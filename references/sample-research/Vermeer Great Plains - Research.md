# Vermeer Great Plains: CAIO strategic growth research brief

Vermeer Great Plains is a **100% employee-owned (ESOP)** Vermeer equipment dealer operating five locations across Kansas, Oklahoma, and western Missouri, with roughly **~80 employees and an estimated ~$22M in annual revenue**. The company serves tree care, underground utility, recycling, pipeline, and forage customers — a typical mid-market industrial equipment dealer facing an industry in cyclical downturn, acute technician shortages, and early-stage AI disruption from OEMs. This brief provides the foundation for a 10-slide AI strategic growth report with SG&A focus.

---

## Company profile and ownership structure

Vermeer Great Plains, Inc., headquartered in Olathe, Kansas, is an **authorized independent dealer** of Vermeer Corporation (Pella, Iowa) — not a subsidiary or company-owned store. The company was established circa **1974** (per its own website; LinkedIn cites 1964, likely reflecting an earlier informal founding). Former president **Art Swank**, a founding member of the North American Vermeer Dealers Association, retired in April 2017 and sold the company to its employees via an ESOP.

The ESOP is central to company identity. Its core values deliberately spell "ESOP": **E**xceptional service, **S**tewardship and integrity, **O**wnership mentality, **P**rofit focus. Employees gain shares annually and can sell them back upon retirement.

## Financial benchmarks for equipment dealers

Vermeer Great Plains operates in a segment where margins are thin and structural. These benchmarks provide the SG&A framing for an AI strategy.

**Profitability by department** reveals where value concentrates. New wholegoods carry just **4.5–7% gross margins**, used wholegoods **8.8–15.5%**, parts **30–35%**, and service labor **42–60%+**. The aftermarket (parts + service combined) averages roughly **42% gross margin** — nearly 10x the margin on new equipment. Yet industry trends show aftermarket revenue share declining from **~26% of dealer sales in 2018 to ~20% by late 2023** as wholegoods prices surged. This margin mix shift is the single most important financial dynamic for AI strategy: every dollar moved from wholegoods dependency toward aftermarket revenue is transformative.

**Dealer-level profitability** runs thin. Average profit from operations peaked at **$759,000 per location (3.7% of revenue)** in 2021 per NAEDA. The industry target for a healthy dealership is **5%+ operating margin**. SG&A (fixed expenses) typically runs **10–11% of gross sales for large dealers and 14–16% for smaller dealers** — with salary and benefits as the largest cost bucket. Wholesale trade median SG&A is **17.42%** of sales. For a ~$22M dealer like Vermeer Great Plains, SG&A likely falls in the **14–17% range** (⚠️ estimated), meaning roughly **$3–4M in annual SG&A** is the optimization target.

The **absorption rate** — aftermarket gross profit covering total fixed expenses — benchmarks at **80–100%**. Large dealers achieved 82.7% in 2022; smaller/mid-size dealers typically range **66–75%**. Reaching 100% absorption (where parts and service alone cover all overhead) is the gold standard.

## Industry headwinds creating urgency for AI

The agricultural equipment sector is in a **significant cyclical downturn**. New dealer sales fell **15.5% in 2024** and are projected to drop another **18.6% in 2025**. Large equipment categories (4WD tractors, combines) plummeted **35–45% year-over-year**. U.S. net farm income is down **22.6% from its 2022 peak**. Meanwhile, **40% of dealers** report above-target inventory levels, and interest expense on floorplan financing has increased "dramatically" per NAEDA's 2025 study.

The **technician crisis** is existential. Some **65.5% of shops are understaffed** in 2025, with an average **19.3% of positions vacant**. The pipeline math is brutal: **5,000 technicians enter** the workforce annually while **8,000 retire**. The average diesel technician is **42 years old**, and over **50% are above 45**. Enrollment in diesel training programs has dropped **~12% since 2019**. Each unfilled technician position costs roughly **$5,000–$10,000 per month** in lost productivity, and a single technician can generate approximately **$300,000 in annual income** (labor + parts). Turnover runs **16.5%** industry-wide, nearly doubling to **28%** at truck dealers.

**OEM margin pressure** compounds the challenge. Equipment rollover programs squeeze dealer margins toward zero on new units. Dealers must absorb used equipment losses as auction values decline. Digital transformation remains fragmented — dealers juggle **15+ browser tabs** across disconnected OEM portals, and legacy ERP systems create persistent data silos between sales, parts, and service.

## Competitor AI adoption is accelerating

OEM-level AI investment is reshaping expectations across the dealer channel. **John Deere's See & Spray** technology saved **31 million gallons of herbicide** across 5+ million acres in 2025, delivering customer payback in ~1.5 years. Deere invested **$555M** acquiring Blue River Technology and Bear Flag Robotics for computer vision and autonomy. **CNH Industrial** deployed an AI Tech Assistant to **300+ dealer groups** for diagnostic support, and every member of CNH's global leadership team is running at least one generative or agentic AI pilot. **AGCO** targets **$2B in precision ag sales by 2029** and launched PTx FarmENGAGE, a mixed-fleet digital management platform.

At the dealer level, AI shifted from "What is AI?" to **"How can it fix this?"** by 2025. Emerging use cases include AI-powered service diagnostics (reducing 40-minute manual PDF searches to instant answers), technician augmentation (AI completing notes and recommending parts), inventory reconciliation, and predictive maintenance via telematics. The key barrier remains **data quality** — "If the data isn't good, then the AI is no good," as one industry technology leader noted. System integration across siloed DMS, CRM, and OEM portals is the foundational prerequisite.

## Operational pain points ripe for AI intervention

For a dealer like Vermeer Great Plains, six pain points map directly to AI-addressable SG&A optimization:

- **Parts inventory management**: Over 60% of dealers report tracking difficulties across tens of thousands of SKUs. AGCO's AI-driven Managed Dealer Inventory program lifted customer-facing fill rates from **60–70% to over 85%** across 1,750+ dealers. Parts turn rates of **~2.5x annually** and DIO of **60–100+ days** signal significant working capital trapped in inventory.

- **Service scheduling and technician efficiency**: The average technician runs only **35–40% collectible efficiency** without management intervention versus a **60%+ target**. At an $80/hour shop rate, the gap between 40% and 80% efficiency represents hundreds of thousands in unrealized revenue per location annually. AI-driven scheduling can match technician skills to jobs, pre-stage parts, and optimize bay utilization.

- **Technician workforce augmentation**: With the shortage structural and worsening, AI diagnostic tools can elevate junior technicians to perform at higher levels. Knowledge capture systems can preserve retiring experts' institutional knowledge. Remote diagnostic support and AR-guided repair extend existing capacity.

- **Seasonal demand forecasting**: Dual seasonality (spring planting, fall harvest) creates cash flow volatility and inventory planning complexity. ML models integrating weather data, crop prices, farm income projections, and historical patterns can materially improve forecast accuracy for both parts stocking and whole goods positioning.

- **Customer data unification**: Sales, parts, service, and rental departments commonly operate in silos. Approximately **23% of dealership calls go unanswered** and only **~25% capture complete customer information**. A unified customer platform with AI-powered proactive outreach (service reminders based on machine hours, automated follow-up sequences) directly impacts retention and aftermarket revenue.

- **Warranty and quoting automation**: Warranty claims consume **2–5% of manufacturer revenue** and represent a significant administrative burden at the dealer level. Multi-brand pricing complexity and inconsistent labor quoting across locations add further friction that AI can standardize.

## Conclusion

Vermeer Great Plains presents a classic mid-market dealer AI opportunity. An **ESOP-owned, ~$22M revenue, 80-person company** with five locations cannot afford enterprise-scale technology investments, yet faces the same structural pressures — **declining equipment sales, a worsening technician crisis, thin margins, and data fragmentation** — as dealers ten times its size. The strategic lever is clear: shift margin mix toward the **42%+ gross margin aftermarket** and away from **4.5–7% wholegoods dependency** through AI-enabled parts optimization, service efficiency, and proactive customer engagement. With SG&A likely running **$3–4M annually** (⚠️ estimated), even modest AI-driven efficiency gains in scheduling, inventory, and customer communication represent meaningful EBITDA improvement. The company's ESOP culture — with its explicit "ownership mentality" and "profit focus" values — may prove to be its strongest asset in driving AI adoption, as employee-owners have direct financial incentive to embrace tools that improve profitability.
