# DLR Group: Comprehensive Firm Intelligence Report

**DLR Group is a $537M, 100% employee-owned integrated design firm that nearly doubled its revenue in five years, ranking among the top 20 U.S. architecture practices.** Founded in 1966 in Omaha, Nebraska, the firm has grown through aggressive M&A and organic expansion to employ roughly 1,800 people across 35+ offices globally. Its ESOP structure — fully employee-owned since 2005 — is a genuine competitive differentiator in an industry where many large rivals have turned to private equity or public markets. DLR Group's diversified sector portfolio spanning justice, K-12 education, sports, hospitality, healthcare, and federal work insulates it from single-sector downturns, though the broader AEC market faces persistent headwinds from a contracting Architecture Billings Index and acute labor shortages.

## Founding, ownership, and how the firm is organized

DLR Group was founded on April 1, 1966, by architects Irving Dana and Bill Larson and engineer Jim Roubal, who left Leo A. Daly to start their own practice as Dana Larson Roubal and Associates. The firm rebranded to DLR Group in 1996. It established its ESOP in **1978** — among the earliest in the region — and became **100% employee-owned in 2005** as an S-ESOP (Subchapter S corporation), meaning flow-through taxation with no external shareholders.

The firm operates with a deliberately **distributed leadership model** — there is no centralized corporate headquarters. While Omaha, NE (6457 Frances St, Ste 200) serves as the registered origin, all executive, operations, and design leadership is spread across offices. The organizational hierarchy runs from Associates through Senior Associates, Principals, Senior Principals (fewer than 2% of staff), to Managing Principals. A Board of Directors is elected by employee-owners, supported by a seven-member Advisory Board meeting quarterly. In 2025, the firm made **192 leadership appointments**: 3 senior principals, 27 principals, 51 senior associates, and 111 associates. The legal structure includes DLR Holding Co. as the parent entity, with operating subsidiaries DLR Group Inc. and DLR Group of DC, P.C.

Employee-owner investment grew **126.7%** from 2020 to 2025, and stock value has reportedly appreciated roughly 15% annually over the last decade. In 2022 alone, employees purchased approximately $10M in DLR Holding Company stock. Nearly half of employee-owners identify as women.

This integration is a genuine operational model, not merely marketing — project teams pull from multiple disciplines housed within the firm rather than subcontracting.

**Core disciplines** include Architecture, Engineering (structural, mechanical, electrical, civil), Interiors (175+ designers led by Audrey Koehn), and Planning. **Specialty services** round out the offering: Acoustic Design, Experiential Graphic Design, High Performance Design (sustainability/energy), Landscape Architecture, Lighting Design, Preservation, Reality Capture (3D laser scanning and digital twins), Renewables, Science + Technology (lab design), and Theater Planning.

## The AEC industry faces a contracting billings cycle and deepening labor crisis

The broader architecture and engineering industry is navigating significant headwinds that contextualize DLR Group's performance. The **AIA Architecture Billings Index** — the primary leading indicator of nonresidential construction activity with a 9–12 month lead — has been **below 50 (contraction) in 35 of the last 38 months** as of December 2025. December 2025 ABI stood at 48.5. Average firm backlog is **6.3 months** (8.6 months at large firms with $5M+ annual billings). The AIA Consensus Forecast projects modest **3.9% growth in 2026**, but meaningful recovery remains uncertain.

**Labor shortages** rank as the industry's most acute challenge. An estimated **499,000 new construction workers are needed by 2026**, while 41% of the current workforce is projected to retire by 2031 and only 7% of job seekers consider construction careers. Architecture firms report an 81% concern rate for recruitment, and the "Missing Middle" — a dearth of mid-career professionals — is the second-most-anticipated disruption. Construction wages rose 4.2% year-over-year as of August 2025.

Additional headwinds include tariff uncertainty raising material costs, persistent elevated construction costs deterring client investment, federal spending cuts (277,000 federal positions eliminated in 2025), and lingering interest rate effects. **Growth sectors** offering counterbalance include data centers and AI infrastructure (explosive demand), healthcare (aging demographics), K-12 education (Sun Belt population growth), Bipartisan Infrastructure Law projects entering peak delivery, and adaptive reuse.

Industry financial benchmarks for context: typical A&E firm net profit margins run **10–20%** (top quartile ~22%), EBITDA multiples average **4.28x** (2025 Zweig Report), overhead rates average 162% at architecture firms, median utilization is 61%, and the average collection period is 81 days. The global AEC market reached **$16.3 trillion in 2025**, growing at 6% CAGR.

## AI adoption is exploratory but systematically organized

DLR Group's AI and technology innovation posture is best characterized as **early-stage but organizationally intentional**. The firm has a dedicated Design Technology team led by Principal William Carney, a recognized Autodesk expert, and a formalized Reality Capture service line — uncommon among integrated design firms at this scale.

The most substantive AI initiative is **Evolution of Campus (EOC) 5.0**, a multi-year proprietary research program launched in February 2025 spanning 240 institutions and 6.5 million students. It explores AI-powered building systems analysis, predictive maintenance, automated building controls, and sustainability optimization for higher education campuses. The **DATA STREAMS** project, funded through an internal Professional Development Grant, explores real-time building data visualization. The firm is also exploring generative AI applications in design including Gaussian Splats-to-Revit workflows and LLM-generated bubble diagrams.

DLR Group Middle East director Henrique Dias stated publicly: "We're investing heavily in digital capability and new technologies. From AI-powered analysis to immersive visualisation, our tools are becoming smarter, faster, and more intuitive." However, compared to computational design leaders like SOM's Digital Design Group, Zaha Hadid's ZHACODE, or KPF's proprietary tools, DLR Group's AI adoption remains more applied-research than production-integrated. The firm's structured training program — with measurable outcomes including tripled software adoption and 17–19% culture index improvements — provides infrastructure for scaling AI tools once they mature. The firm is **CMMC Level I compliant** and pursuing Level II, reflecting cybersecurity investment necessary for federal work.

## Conclusion

DLR Group is a well-managed, fast-growing integrated design firm that has executed a disciplined growth strategy to nearly double revenue in five years. Three insights stand out from this analysis. First, the **ESOP structure is not merely a cultural talking point** — the 126.7% growth in employee-owner investment and ~15% annual stock appreciation create genuine retention advantages in an industry facing acute labor shortages. Second, the firm's **M&A playbook is unusually coherent** for an architecture practice: each acquisition clearly fills a capability gap (healthcare/Tsoi Kobus, luxury interiors/BraytonHughes) or geographic gap (Atlanta/CCCA, Boston/Tsoi Kobus) rather than pursuing scale for its own sake. Third, while DLR Group's **AI and technology adoption is not industry-leading**, its systematic approach to training (measurable KPIs, firm-wide skills assessments, dedicated learning leads) positions it to absorb new tools faster than peers once production-ready AI applications emerge. The primary risk factors are exposure to federal spending uncertainty (government shutdowns, DOGE cuts) and the prolonged ABI contraction that has now persisted for over three years.
